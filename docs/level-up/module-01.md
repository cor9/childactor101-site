I manage a roster of around 25-30 youth actors and a sprinkle of adults. Through determination, patience and hard work my clients have begun making their mark in the industry.

I have managed to carve out a schedule that allows me to Talent Manage full time and coach kids online as well as teach one class a day. This makes me creatively fulfilled and happily connected to my work and the youth out there chasing their dreams.

## What It Takes To Be A Successful Child Actor

A lot of parents ask the same question: "How do I get my child started in the entertainment industry?" Often parents ask because they have a hunch their kid is unique. They may have an adorable and expressive kindergartener with an impressive vocabulary, a child who got rave reviews in a school production, or a funny preteen who performs online all day. A dream of fame is born. A dream that their kid will book a series regular role on a hit sitcom within months of starting out.

Unfortunately, many of these parents are in for a rude awakening. Often an expensive and heartbreaking one.

It takes a special kind of kid to succeed as an actor. A strong desire or a sassy attitude is not enough.

Being cute is not a prerequisite to an acting career, nor is it enough. Television and film now seek a diverse range of humans. The stereotypical cute kid market is over-saturated and extremely competitive.

A young actor needs to be self-confident and thick-skinned. They have to know they are valuable and worthy beyond how many social media followers they have. They need to be able to take criticism and rejection and learn from it.

Actors must be intelligent and have superb reading skills. Often actors research, study, and observe many subjects to understand a script or a character. Many also have a very high emotional aptitude. They observe and analyze human behavior. They connect to material and reflect on emotions within themselves with a goal of reaching empathy.

Successful youth actors respect life experience and understand that it is key to bringing truth to a scene. Performance notes and redirects fuel them because they see adjustments as another exciting piece of the puzzle.

Kids that book jobs are genuine. They are authentic and well-intentioned people. Their slates do not sound fake or over-rehearsed. Trying too hard is a turnoff. Desperation is a repellent.

Those performers that rise to the top often have the "It Factor." That factor is charisma. The ability to be charming and likable. The power to build a fan base from a single meeting. Charismatic kids can talk to adults with ease, stay positive, know their strengths, and turn anxiety into laughable moments.

Parents need to be able to discern when their child is giving unnatural line readings. There is no room for pretend in acting. Performances should be grounded and in the moment.

Youth actors who make it in TV and film do not play it safe. They take chances to stand out. They bring a strong point of view to scenes and make specific choices.

Successful comedy requires fearlessness, physicality, energy, and commitment. Dramatic acting requires listening, active thought, and emotional access.

These are kids who train like an Olympic gold medal is at stake. They do not need to be bribed to memorize lines or self tape an audition. They are motivated, passionate, and inspired by the craft itself.

These kids are often well rounded but not over-committed. They are patient enough to handle the marathon that the career usually becomes.

Children who achieve longevity can display grace under pressure. They have the maturity to hold their own as the only kid on a set full of adults.

Families also need the ability to support and invest in the career. There are no shortcuts to Hollywood. Overnight success is an anomaly. To be competitive, actors need quality materials and consistent training from experienced instructors.

The qualities above are vital to success. Parents need to take a real critical look at what their child is capable of and seek feedback from unbiased, knowledgeable evaluators.

It is admirable to support these ambitions, but it is foolish to push children into something they are not ready for. If the raw material is there, then parents should be the supportive rock and moral compass that helps the child move forward.

## Unions

Unions play an essential role in protecting the rights and well-being of child actors.

Key unions include:

1. SAG-AFTRA, representing performers in film, television, and radio.
2. Actors' Equity Association, representing actors and stage managers in live theater.

These unions negotiate contracts, set minimum standards for wages and working conditions, and often include child-specific protections related to education, hours, and supervision.

## Non-Union Work

Non-union work can include independent films, student films, web series, local commercials, community theater, non-profit projects, and more.

Important factors for parents to consider:

1. Working conditions
2. Compensation
3. Contractual agreements
4. Networking value
5. Future union eligibility

## Joining SAG-AFTRA

Common pathways discussed in this module:

1. Principal performer eligibility
2. Taft-Hartley eligibility
3. Voucher-based eligibility
4. Reciprocal membership from affiliated unions
5. Eligibility through new media agreements

The module's practical advice: wait as long as possible before joining the union unless joining clearly serves the actor's current stage and opportunities.

## Talent Representation

### Talent Agents

Talent agents submit actors for roles, help secure auditions, and negotiate work. They are licensed to legally procure work and usually work on commission.

### Talent Managers

Talent managers provide broader career guidance, help with decision-making, and are often more accessible for long-term strategy and development.

### Regional Agents

Regional agents can help actors build experience and momentum without requiring an immediate move to Los Angeles or New York. Cities discussed in the module include Atlanta, Chicago, New York, Austin, New Orleans, Albuquerque, and Miami.

### Voice Over Agents

Voice over agents specialize in opportunities requiring vocal performance, including animation, games, and commercials.

### Print/Modeling Agents

Print and modeling agents focus on catalogs, ads, campaigns, and other visual-media work. This can be a separate specialty lane and is not limited to conventionally attractive children.

## Finding Representation

### Don't Pay to Play

Never pay upfront fees to talent reps. Legitimate agents make money when the actor makes money.

### Scam Showcases

Avoid showcases and events that rely on high-pressure promises and large upfront payments.

### Do Your Homework

Seek recommendations, check credentials, and verify whether a representative is properly licensed and reputable.

### When You Are Ready, Submit

This module positions submission as a strategic step that should come after honest preparation, not before.
